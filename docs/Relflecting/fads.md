## Nothing Changes

Did I mention that I have been working in the IT industry for over 40 years? I guess it's something I tend not to mention .... much As I head towards retirement and (*hopefully*) quieter days, I feel I have earned the right to look at new developments in the world of technology and say, as many before m have: "There is nothing new". 

To be fair, this isn't quite true, but I'll come to that at some point. However, for the most part of my career I have seen lots of instances of something new and shiny, maybe the *"Best thing since sliced bread"*, yet oin inspection it has proved to be anything but. The many instances make for good topics to discuss over a suitable libation, but I'll start by looking at the general pattern.

## The pattern of a Fad - or is it a "Hype-Cycle"?

### The start

It is difficult to find a starting point for any hyped technology, generally the concepts have been around for a long period before someone, somewhere puts it to novel use. It is that novel use that drives the hype for several reasons.

You maybe don't believe me, but try a couple of examples:

- ***Big Data*** - came to wider attention when a Silton Valley start-up combined the performance of in-memory database, with a large data set of test posts harvested from a social media provider to show customer sentiment.

    The technology wasn't new, but larger memory devices made it economic to put data tables in memory. The availability of APIs by social media providers and the availability of high-bandwidth internet connections, made it possible to harvest textual data by hashtags on a scale never imagined

- ***Cloud Compute*** - was made viable by maturing virtual machine software running on processors becoming available with multiple-cores able to process massive workloads, and again that internet bandwidth.

I could keep going bu that would create a litany that doesn't really help the case yet

### Confusion

I posit that the key to these periods of hype is the initial confusion that abounds, and fosters serious FOMO, firstly on teh part of supply-side who don't want to be seen as behind the curve, and then on the part of consuming organisations who may feel that there is competitive advantage at risk of being lost.

Hindsight, they say, is always 20/20, but for each period of boom, there has been a significant amount of mis-understanding around the real nature of any proposition and what it represents. My personal experience on the part of the *big Data* example above was being told of the power of understanding customer sentiment ... and that is as true today as it was then. What was either mis-understood, or maybe intentionally left out of teh conversations, was that to get the scale of data needed to do the content analysis required large populations of tech-savvy customers using Twitter to express their views.
